# Macro Framework

> The structural model for understanding how stress originates, propagates, and resolves in the global financial system.

---

## The Core Transmission Chain

The global financial system does not fail randomly. Stress follows a recognizable transmission path — from the environment, through the monetary system, into funding and liquidity, then through debt markets and confidence, ultimately reaching asset markets and triggering either recovery or deleveraging.

```
Environment
  → Central Bank / Money Creation
  → Funding
  → Liquidity
  → Debt Market
  → Confidence
  → Asset Market
  → Deleveraging
  → Financial Immune System / Treatment Response
```

Understanding *where in this chain* stress is originating and *how far it has propagated* is the fundamental diagnostic task of this system.

---

## Each Stage Explained

### Environment
The conditions that surround and shape the financial system: geopolitical events, energy markets, supply chains, demographic trends, fiscal trajectories. The environment cannot be controlled — it must be observed and understood.

### Central Bank / Money Creation
Central banks control the price and quantity of money. Their decisions about interest rates, reserve requirements, asset purchases (QE/QT), and forward guidance set the fundamental conditions for everything downstream. A shift in central bank posture — tightening or loosening — begins a chain reaction through the entire system.

### Funding
Funding is the mechanism by which financial actors obtain the resources they need to operate. Short-term funding markets (repo, commercial paper, interbank lending) are particularly sensitive. When funding conditions tighten — costs rise, availability shrinks, counterparty risk increases — stress begins to appear.

### Liquidity
Liquidity is broader than funding. It encompasses the ease with which assets can be bought and sold, the bid-ask spreads in markets, the availability of credit. A liquidity crisis occurs when funding stress reaches a scale that freezes normal market function.

### Debt Market
The debt market — sovereign bonds, corporate bonds, structured credit — reflects and amplifies financial conditions. Rising yields signal stress in sovereign funding. Widening credit spreads signal stress in corporate and financial sector funding. Failed treasury auctions signal a crisis of confidence in sovereign debt.

### Confidence
Confidence is the intangible that holds the financial system together. Banks lend to each other because they trust each other. Investors buy bonds because they trust the issuer. When confidence fractures — when trust breaks — the financial system can seize up very rapidly, far faster than any change in economic fundamentals.

### Asset Market
Asset markets — equities, real estate, commodities, foreign exchange — are downstream of everything above. When the tree is healthy, assets appreciate. When the tree is stressed, assets reprice to reflect that stress. Asset market movements are *symptoms*, not *causes*.

### Deleveraging
Deleveraging is the crisis phase — the forced unwinding of leveraged positions when margin calls, funding withdrawal, or confidence collapse forces actors to sell assets and reduce debt simultaneously. Deleveraging is self-reinforcing: falling prices trigger more selling, which drives prices lower, which triggers more selling.

### Financial Immune System / Treatment Response
The system's response to crisis: central bank intervention, government fiscal response, international coordination. These treatments can be effective — but they also have side effects. Quantitative easing treats the immediate crisis but can create conditions for future instability. Understanding the treatment history is essential for understanding the patient's current condition.

## Systemic Stress Scenarios

### War and Supply Chain Shock — External Trauma
War and supply chain disruption enter the system as external shocks (Storms in the tree metaphor). They do not originate in the financial system, but they interact with existing vulnerabilities:

- War drives energy prices → inflation → central bank tightening → funding stress.
- Supply chain disruption reduces output → stagflation risk → difficult monetary policy choices.
- A financially fragile system is far more vulnerable to external shock than a healthy one.

### Fed + BOJ Tightening — Global Funding Shock
When the Federal Reserve and Bank of Japan tighten policy simultaneously, the global dollar funding system comes under acute stress:

- The Fed raises rates → the cost of dollar funding rises globally.
- The BOJ ends yield curve control or raises rates → capital flows back to Japan → global dollar liquidity drains.
- This combination creates a global funding shock that reverberates through every market.

### Yen Carry Trade Unwind
The yen carry trade is one of the largest structural trades in global finance: borrowing yen at near-zero rates, converting to dollars (or other currencies), and investing in higher-yielding assets globally.

When the BOJ tightens or the yen strengthens for other reasons, carry traders must reverse this position:
- Sell higher-yielding assets.
- Buy yen.
- Repay yen debt.

A rapid, large-scale yen carry trade unwind creates simultaneous selling pressure across multiple asset classes globally — a cascade that is difficult to contain.

### Dollar Short Squeeze
A dollar short squeeze occurs when entities that have borrowed in dollars (effectively short dollars) face rising dollar costs simultaneously:

- Dollar strengthens → emerging market dollar debt becomes more expensive.
- Dollar funding tightens → borrowing costs rise sharply.
- Entities forced to buy dollars → dollar strengthens further → feedback loop.

A dollar short squeeze is one of the most dangerous conditions in the global financial system because it affects every entity with dollar-denominated debt simultaneously.

### Shadow Banking Liquidity Freeze
Shadow banks — money market funds, hedge funds, private credit, repo market participants — are outside the formal banking system. They are not backed by deposit guarantees or direct central bank support.

During stress, shadow banking liquidity can freeze very rapidly:
- Money market funds gate redemptions.
- Repo market funding disappears.
- Hedge funds face margin calls and are forced to sell assets.
- The contagion spreads to the formal banking system.

Shadow banking freezes often precede or accelerate formal banking crises.

## Reading the Framework

This framework is the lens through which the owner reads all incoming data.

When SOFR spikes, the question is: *where in the funding → liquidity chain is this stress originating?*

When gold surges, the question is: *what does this say about confidence in the monetary system?*

When the US 30Y yield rises, the question is: *is this a growth expectation, an inflation expectation, or a confidence signal in sovereign debt?*

The framework prevents the owner from treating any single signal as isolated. Every signal is a data point in a system-wide diagnosis.
