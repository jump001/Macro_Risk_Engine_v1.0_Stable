# Glossary

> Definitions of key terms used throughout this system. Understanding the vocabulary is part of understanding the system.

---

## Core Framework Terms

### Living Knowledge System
This project's name for the complete intellectual infrastructure — the combination of the Risk Engine (quantitative analysis), the Financial Medical Record (diagnostic framework), and the Knowledge artifacts (journals, reflections, hypotheses, case studies). The system is "living" because it grows and evolves alongside the owner's understanding.

### Financial Medical Record
The application of a medical diagnostic framework to the global financial system. The global financial system is treated as a patient with measurable vital signs, diagnosable conditions, and treatable crises. See [docs/04-financial-medical-record.md](./04-financial-medical-record.md).

### Tree of Understanding
The structural metaphor used to understand the global financial system — from Soil (central banks) through Roots (credit system), Sap (liquidity), Trunk (global funding), Branches (capital flows), to Leaves (asset markets). See [docs/05-tree-of-understanding.md](./05-tree-of-understanding.md).

### Research Studio
The design philosophy for the application's user interface — a quiet private research environment, not a financial terminal or news dashboard. See [docs/03-research-studio.md](./03-research-studio.md).

## Macro Financial Terms

### Liquidity
The ease with which money flows through the financial system — how readily assets can be bought and sold, and how available funding is. High liquidity means markets function smoothly, spreads are narrow, and capital is accessible. Low liquidity means markets are impaired, spreads widen, and accessing funding becomes difficult or expensive. In the tree metaphor, liquidity is the sap.

### Funding
The mechanism by which financial institutions obtain the resources they need to operate. Short-term funding markets — repo (repurchase agreements), commercial paper, interbank lending — are particularly important. When funding conditions deteriorate, it is often the first sign of systemic stress.

### SOFR (Secured Overnight Financing Rate)
The benchmark interest rate for overnight US dollar lending secured by Treasury collateral (repo market). SOFR replaced LIBOR as the primary dollar funding benchmark. Elevated SOFR indicates stress in the short-term dollar funding market — the equivalent of rising blood pressure in the financial system. SOFR stress is the first indicator in the Golden Signal.

### DXY (US Dollar Index)
A measure of the US dollar's strength against a basket of major currencies (Euro, Japanese Yen, British Pound, Canadian Dollar, Swedish Krona, Swiss Franc). A rising DXY indicates the dollar is strengthening globally. Sustained DXY strength — particularly above 105 — creates stress for entities with dollar-denominated debt outside the United States, and is a sign of global dollar shortage.

### US 30Y (US 30-Year Treasury Yield)
The interest rate on 30-year US government bonds. The 30Y yield represents the market's assessment of the long-term cost of US government borrowing, inflation expectations, and the sustainability of long-term debt. A breakout above key technical levels is a signal of long-term debt stress and loss of confidence in long-term fiscal stability.

### Treasury Auction
The process by which the US government issues new debt — selling Treasury bills, notes, and bonds to the public. Auction results reveal the health of demand for US government debt. A "weak" auction (low bid-to-cover ratio, high tail) indicates that buyers demanded higher yields than expected, which is a stress signal for the debt market. Treasury auction weakness is one of the five Golden Signal components.

### Gold
Gold is a monetary metal that functions as a store of value and safe haven. Gold breakouts typically indicate rising systemic fear, declining confidence in fiat currencies, or expectations of monetary system stress. It is an indicator of confidence stress rather than economic activity.

### MOVE Index
The Merrill Lynch Option Volatility Estimate — a measure of volatility in the US Treasury bond market, derived from options pricing. The MOVE Index is to the bond market what VIX is to the equity market. Elevated MOVE (above ~130) indicates that bond market participants are pricing in significant uncertainty about future interest rates — a sign of systemic stress.

### VIX (CBOE Volatility Index)
A measure of the expected volatility of the S&P 500 over the next 30 days, derived from options prices. Often called the "fear gauge." Elevated VIX (above ~25 sustained) indicates elevated fear and uncertainty in equity markets. In the tree metaphor, elevated VIX is a symptom visible in the leaves.

## Crisis and Stress Terms

### Yen Carry Trade
A financial strategy of borrowing Japanese yen at very low interest rates (Japan has historically maintained near-zero rates), converting to another currency (typically US dollars), and investing in higher-yielding assets. When the Bank of Japan raises rates or the yen strengthens, carry traders must unwind — selling their higher-yielding assets and buying yen simultaneously, creating sudden selling pressure across multiple global asset classes.

### Dollar Short Squeeze
A condition in which entities that have effectively "sold" dollars (borrowed in dollars and deployed capital elsewhere) are forced to simultaneously buy dollars to repay debt or meet margin calls. Because the dollar is the world's reserve currency, many entities globally have dollar liabilities. A dollar short squeeze can create a cascading strengthening of the dollar that damages all dollar-borrowing entities simultaneously.

### Financial Immune System
The set of mechanisms that the financial system deploys in response to stress: central bank intervention (the first responder), government fiscal measures (the sustained healer), market self-correction (the sometimes painful clearing process), and international coordination (the collective global response). The immune system can be effective — but like a biological immune system, it can also cause collateral damage (inflation from QE, moral hazard from bailouts).

### Deleveraging
The process — often forced and cascading — in which financial actors reduce their debt and leverage simultaneously. Deleveraging is self-reinforcing: falling asset prices trigger margin calls and forced selling, which drives prices lower, which triggers more margin calls. Deleveraging events are among the most dangerous conditions in the financial system because they are difficult to stop once initiated.

### QE (Quantitative Easing)
A central bank intervention in which the central bank creates new money and uses it to purchase financial assets (typically government bonds and sometimes mortgage-backed securities). QE injects liquidity into the financial system, lowers long-term interest rates, and supports asset prices. It is a treatment for financial stress — effective in the short term, but with potential long-term side effects (asset price inflation, moral hazard).

### Swap Line
An agreement between two central banks to exchange their currencies at a fixed rate for a specified period. The Federal Reserve has swap lines with many major central banks. In a crisis, swap lines allow foreign central banks to provide dollar liquidity to their domestic banks without needing to use their own dollar reserves. A swap line activation is an emergency measure — the financial equivalent of an emergency blood transfusion.

### Yield Curve Control (YCC)
A policy in which a central bank commits to buying however much government debt is necessary to keep interest rates at a specific target level across the yield curve (or a specific part of it). YCC is an extreme intervention — the financial equivalent of life support — used when normal monetary policy is insufficient to control rates.

## Risk Engine Terms

### Risk Score
A composite numerical measure (0–100) representing the current level of systemic stress in the global financial system, calculated by the Risk Engine from weighted indicator readings.

### Golden Signal
A specific configuration of five simultaneous stress conditions that, when all present together, represents a high-confidence systemic stress signal: SOFR stress + Treasury auction weakness + US 30Y breakout + Gold breakout + Rising volatility (MOVE/VIX).

### Risk State
The categorical health classification output by the Risk Engine: HEALTHY, WATCH, FRAGILE, PRE_CRISIS, CRITICAL, or DELEVERAGING.
